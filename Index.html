01
Explain the concept of opportunity cost with a simple real-life example. Illustrate using a Production Possibility Frontier (PPF).
Definition

Opportunity Cost is the value of the next best alternative forgone when making a choice. It is the hidden cost of every decision — what you give up to get something else.

Real-Life Example

Example

You have 2 hours. You can either study for your exam OR watch a movie. If you choose to study, the opportunity cost is the enjoyment of the movie you gave up. If you choose the movie, the opportunity cost is the grade improvement you lost by not studying.

Business Example

A farmer has land to grow either wheat or rice. If he grows wheat, the opportunity cost is the profit he would have earned from rice. Every scarce resource has an opportunity cost.

Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)

The PPF shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce with its available resources and technology.

Guns | 10|* A (10 guns, 0 butter) 8| * 6| * ← PPF Curve 4| * 2| * 0|__________* B (0 guns, 8 butter) Butter • Points ON the curve = Efficient (full resource use) • Points INSIDE the curve = Inefficient (resources wasted) • Points OUTSIDE the curve = Impossible (beyond capacity) • Moving from A→B shows OPPORTUNITY COST of butter = guns given up

Key Characteristics of PPF

Downward Sloping

To produce more of one good, you must give up some of the other — resources are scarce.

Concave Shape

Due to the Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost — resources are not perfectly adaptable between uses.

Shifts Outward

Technological improvement or more resources shift the PPF outward (economic growth).

Trade-Off

Every point on the PPF represents a trade-off: society must choose how to allocate limited resources. There is no "free lunch" — getting more of one thing requires sacrificing another.

Exam Tip Always draw the PPF diagram, label axes with two goods, mark points inside/on/outside, and explain opportunity cost as movement along the curve. Mention increasing opportunity cost due to resource specialization.
02
Analyze how an increase in demand affects equilibrium price and quantity in a competitive market.
Key Concept

Market Equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied (Qd = Qs). The price at this point is called the equilibrium price.

What Happens When Demand Increases?

An increase in demand means consumers want MORE of a good at EVERY price level. The demand curve shifts rightward (D₁ → D₂).

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Demand Shifts Right

At the original price (P₁), consumers now want more than before → a shortage appears (Qd > Qs).

Step 2: Shortage Creates Pressure

Sellers notice they are running out of stock. Buyers compete, willing to pay more.

Step 3: Price Rises

Price increases from P₁ to P₂. Higher price discourages some buyers (Qd falls) and encourages more production (Qs rises).

Step 4: New Equilibrium

A new equilibrium is reached at higher price (P₂) and higher quantity (Q₂).

Price | S P₂|......../........* NEW equilibrium P₁|......*../........ | / / | / / |___/D₁/D₂_______Quantity Q₁ Q₂ RESULT: ↑ Demand → ↑ Price AND ↑ Quantity

Causes of Demand Increase (Demand Shifters)

↑ Income ↑ Population ↑ Tastes/Preference ↑ Price of Substitute ↓ Price of Complement Positive Expectations

Conclusion

When demand increases in a competitive market, both equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity rise. This is a key self-correcting mechanism of the free market — price signals guide resources to where they are most valued.

Exam Tip Always draw supply & demand diagram. Show the demand curve shifting right, mark OLD equilibrium (E₁) and NEW equilibrium (E₂). Explain the adjustment process, not just the result.
03
State and explain the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility with an example.
Law Statement

The Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility states that as a consumer consumes more and more units of a commodity, the additional satisfaction (marginal utility) derived from each successive unit goes on decreasing, other things remaining constant.

Key Terms

Utility

The satisfaction or pleasure a consumer gets from consuming a good or service.

Marginal Utility (MU)

The additional utility gained from consuming one more unit. MU = TU₂ − TU₁

Total Utility (TU)

The total satisfaction from all units consumed. TU rises, then falls.

Example: Eating Mangoes

Unit (Mango) Total Utility Marginal Utility Remarks
1st3030Highest satisfaction
2nd5020Still positive
3rd6010Diminishing
4th600Point of Satiation
5th55−5Negative MU (discomfort)

Why Does MU Diminish?

Psychological Reason

The urgency of the want decreases as the want gets satisfied progressively.

Satiation Effect

The first unit satisfies the most intense need; subsequent units satisfy less urgent needs.

Importance / Applications

Explains Downward Sloping Demand

As MU falls, consumers pay less for additional units → lower price = more quantity demanded.

Explains Consumer Surplus

First units give more utility than price paid → consumer surplus exists.

Explains Price Policy

Businesses use this to set pricing for bulk packages (diminishing willingness to pay).

Exam Tip Draw the MU curve (downward sloping) and TU curve (rises then peaks then falls). The point where MU = 0 is where TU is maximum (point of satiation). Always include the table AND diagram.
04
Analyze how price elasticity of demand influences a firm's pricing decision.
Definition

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures how sensitive quantity demanded is to a change in price.

PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded ÷ % Change in Price

Types of Elasticity

Elastic Demand (PED > 1)
  • Consumers very sensitive to price
  • Small price rise → big fall in Qd
  • Examples: luxury goods, branded clothes
  • Firm should LOWER price to gain revenue
Inelastic Demand (PED < 1)
  • Consumers not sensitive to price
  • Big price rise → small fall in Qd
  • Examples: medicine, salt, fuel
  • Firm can RAISE price to gain revenue

How Firms Use PED in Pricing

Revenue Strategy

If PED > 1 (elastic): Cutting price increases revenue because Qd rises proportionally more than price falls.
If PED < 1 (inelastic): Raising price increases revenue because Qd falls proportionally less than price rises.

Market Segmentation / Price Discrimination

Airlines, cinemas charge different prices to different groups based on their elasticity. Students (elastic) get discounts; business travelers (inelastic) pay more.

Advertising & Branding

Firms invest in branding to make demand MORE inelastic — loyal customers are less price sensitive (e.g., Apple iPhone).

Product Positioning

Luxury brands intentionally keep PED inelastic through exclusivity, so they can charge premium prices without losing customers.

Factors That Affect PED

Number of Substitutes Necessity vs. Luxury Time Period Proportion of Income Habit/Addiction
Exam Tip Always connect the type of elasticity (elastic/inelastic) directly to the firm's pricing decision and total revenue. Use the formula, give real business examples (Apple, medicine, airlines).
13
Differentiate between movement along and shift of demand curve.
Movement Along the Curve
  • Caused ONLY by price change
  • Demand curve stays same
  • Movement up = higher price, less Qd
  • Movement down = lower price, more Qd
  • Called "Change in Quantity Demanded"
Shift of the Curve
  • Caused by non-price factors
  • Entire curve moves left or right
  • Right shift = demand increase
  • Left shift = demand decrease
  • Called "Change in Demand"

Non-Price Factors That SHIFT the Demand Curve

Income changes Taste/fashion Population size Price of related goods Consumer expectations Season
Practical Example

Movement: Price of tea rises from ৳20 to ৳30 → consumers buy less tea (movement up the same curve).
Shift: Income of consumers rises → they want more tea at every price → demand curve shifts right.

Exam Tip Draw two diagrams side by side — one showing movement along curve (same curve, different point), one showing shift (new curve position). This visually demonstrates the key difference clearly.
14
Define short-run and long-run production. What is economies of scale?
Short Run
  • At least ONE input is FIXED
  • Cannot change factory size
  • Can change labor/raw materials
  • Governed by Law of Diminishing Returns
  • Fixed cost exists
Long Run
  • ALL inputs are VARIABLE
  • Can change factory, technology, land
  • No fixed costs — all costs variable
  • Returns to scale apply
  • Firm can enter/exit market

Economies of Scale

Definition

Economies of Scale occur when a firm's average cost per unit DECREASES as it increases its scale of production (output) in the long run.

Types of Economies of Scale

Technical Economies

Larger machines and automation reduce per-unit cost. A bigger factory uses specialized machinery more efficiently.

Purchasing Economies

Buying raw materials in bulk gets discounts — lower input cost per unit.

Managerial Economies

Specialists (HR, finance, marketing) can be hired — better management per unit.

Financial Economies

Large firms can borrow at lower interest rates — better credit rating.

Marketing Economies

Advertising cost spread over more units — lower per-unit marketing cost.

Diseconomies of Scale

When a firm grows TOO large, average costs start rising again due to poor communication, coordination problems, and bureaucracy. The LRAC curve is U-shaped.

Exam Tip Draw the Long Run Average Cost (LRAC) curve — U-shaped. Show economies of scale on the falling portion, minimum efficient scale at the bottom, and diseconomies on the rising portion.
15
What is price ceiling? Explain how it creates shortage.
Definition

A Price Ceiling is a government-imposed maximum price set BELOW the equilibrium price. It is designed to make goods affordable for consumers.

How It Creates Shortage — Step by Step

Step 1

Equilibrium price = ৳50. Government sets price ceiling at ৳30 (below equilibrium).

Step 2 — Effect on Demand

At ৳30, price is lower than equilibrium → consumers WANT MORE → Quantity Demanded INCREASES.

Step 3 — Effect on Supply

At ৳30, price is lower → producers earn LESS profit → they REDUCE production → Quantity Supplied DECREASES.

Step 4 — Shortage Emerges

Qd > Qs → Shortage = Qd − Qs. Market cannot self-correct because price cannot rise above the ceiling.

Price | S 50|.....*...... ← Equilibrium 30|--/--*------← Price Ceiling (MAX) | / | |/ SHORTAGE |______*_____ Quantity Qs Qd Shortage = Qd - Qs at ceiling price ৳30

Real-World Examples

Examples

• Rent control in Bangladesh/India — landlords reduce housing supply, causing housing shortage.
• Food price controls during war or famine — reduces farmers' incentive to produce more.
• Electricity price cap — power companies cut supply, causing power shortage.

Consequences of Price Ceiling

Shortage of goods Black markets emerge Lower quality products Long queues/rationing
Exam Tip A price ceiling MUST be set BELOW equilibrium to be effective. If set above, it has no effect. Always draw the diagram showing the ceiling line below equilibrium, and mark the shortage gap.
16
Define perfect competition and monopoly. Mention two differences.
Perfect Competition
  • Many buyers & sellers
  • Identical/homogeneous products
  • No single firm controls price
  • Free entry & exit
  • Price = Marginal Cost
  • Example: agricultural markets
Monopoly
  • Single seller dominates market
  • Unique product, no close substitutes
  • Price maker — controls price
  • Barriers to entry (legal/natural)
  • Price > Marginal Cost
  • Example: utility companies, patents

Two Key Differences

Difference 1: Market Power

In perfect competition, no firm can influence price — they are "price takers." In monopoly, the single firm sets the price — it is a "price maker." This means monopoly can charge above marginal cost, earning abnormal profit.

Difference 2: Consumer Welfare

In perfect competition, output is maximized at lowest price (efficient allocation). In monopoly, output is restricted to maximize profit, leading to higher prices and less consumer surplus — causing deadweight loss to society.

Exam Tip Compare them in a table format in the exam. Mention deadweight loss for monopoly and why governments regulate monopolies to protect consumers.

What is consumer surplus? Explain with example.
Definition

Consumer Surplus is the difference between what a consumer is willing to pay for a good and what they actually pay. It represents the extra benefit consumers receive beyond the price they pay.

Consumer Surplus = Maximum Willingness to Pay − Actual Market Price

Example

Numerical Example

You are willing to pay ৳200 for a book. The market price is ৳120. Your consumer surplus = ৳200 − ৳120 = ৳80. This ৳80 is extra value you gained from the transaction.

Graphical Representation

Price |\ ← Consumer Surplus area (triangle above price, below demand curve) | \ P*| *-------- Equilibrium | \ /S | \ / |_____\__/_____ Quantity Q* Consumer Surplus = Area of triangle above price line & below demand curve = ½ × Base × Height

Why Consumer Surplus Matters

Measures Consumer Welfare

Higher consumer surplus = greater benefit to buyers. Competitive markets generate more surplus than monopolies.

Price Discrimination

Firms try to capture consumer surplus by charging different prices to different buyers (e.g., airline tickets, cinema discounts).

Tax Impact

When government imposes tax, price rises → consumer surplus falls → welfare loss occurs.

Exam Tip Show the triangle on a demand-supply diagram. Also connect consumer surplus to producer surplus to explain total social welfare (Total Welfare = CS + PS).

Define nominal GDP and real GDP. Why is real GDP important?
Nominal GDP
  • GDP measured at CURRENT prices
  • Not adjusted for inflation
  • Can be misleading over time
  • Includes price changes + output changes
  • Formula: Σ (Current Price × Current Quantity)
Real GDP
  • GDP measured at CONSTANT base-year prices
  • Adjusted for inflation/deflation
  • True measure of output growth
  • Only reflects actual output changes
  • Formula: Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator × 100

Numerical Example

Example

In 2020: GDP = ৳1000 billion | In 2023: GDP = ৳1300 billion
Inflation rate 2020→2023 = 20%
Nominal GDP 2023 = ৳1300 billion (includes inflation)
Real GDP 2023 = 1300/1.20 = ৳1083 billion (true output growth)
The economy actually grew by only 8.3%, not 30%.

Why Real GDP is Important

True Economic Growth

Shows whether production actually increased or only prices rose. Without adjusting for inflation, we cannot know if living standards improved.

International Comparison

Allows comparing economic performance across countries with different inflation rates.

Policy Making

Government uses real GDP to decide whether to stimulate or slow the economy. If real GDP falls, recession is occurring.

Living Standards

Real GDP per capita is the best indicator of average living standards over time.

Exam Tip Use a numerical example showing nominal vs real GDP. Explain: "Nominal GDP can rise due to inflation alone — it doesn't mean more goods are produced. Real GDP removes this illusion."
06
Analyze how leakages and injections influence equilibrium income in the circular flow model.
Core Concept

The Circular Flow Model shows how income flows between households and firms in an economy. Equilibrium income exists when total injections = total leakages.

What are Leakages?

Leakages are withdrawals of money FROM the circular flow — they reduce income circulation.

Savings (S) Taxes (T) Imports (M)

When households save money, pay taxes, or spend on imported goods, money leaves the domestic circular flow, reducing national income.

What are Injections?

Injections are additions of money INTO the circular flow — they increase income circulation.

Investment (I) Government Spending (G) Exports (X)

When firms invest, government spends on public services, or foreigners buy our exports, money enters the circular flow, raising national income.

Equilibrium Condition

Equilibrium: S + T + M = I + G + X

Or equivalently: National Income (Y) = C + I + G + (X − M)

How Imbalances Affect the Economy

If Injections > Leakages

National income RISES. Firms produce more, employment increases, economy expands. Risk: Inflation if economy is at full capacity.

If Leakages > Injections

National income FALLS. Firms reduce production, unemployment rises, economy contracts. This can lead to recession.

When Injections = Leakages

Economy is in equilibrium. National income is stable — neither expanding nor contracting.

The Multiplier Effect

How Injections Multiply

Government spends ৳100 crore on building roads. Workers earn income → they spend → shopkeepers earn → they spend → and so on. Final impact on GDP is GREATER than the initial ৳100 crore. This is the multiplier effect (k = 1/MPS or 1/MPW).

Policy Implications

Expansionary Policy

To boost income: increase G (government spending) or cut T (taxes) → more injection → income rises.

Contractionary Policy

To reduce inflation: cut G or raise T → more leakage → income falls.

Exam Tip Draw the 5-sector circular flow diagram (households → firms → banks/govt/foreign sector). Label all 3 leakages (S, T, M) and 3 injections (I, G, X). Explain the equilibrium condition and what happens when balance is disrupted. This is a high-value exam question — give full analysis.

19
What is GDP? Explain three methods of measuring GDP.
Definition

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders during a specific time period (usually one year). It measures a country's economic size and activity.

Method 1: Expenditure Approach

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
C (Consumption)

Household spending on goods and services (food, clothing, entertainment).

I (Investment)

Business spending on capital goods (machinery, buildings, inventory).

G (Government Spending)

Government expenditure on public goods and services (defense, education, healthcare).

X − M (Net Exports)

Exports minus imports. If X > M → trade surplus; if M > X → trade deficit.

Method 2: Income Approach

GDP = Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit + Mixed Income

Sums all factor incomes paid in the economy. Every dollar spent becomes someone's income — wages to workers, rent to landlords, interest to lenders, profit to entrepreneurs.

Method 3: Output/Production Approach

GDP = Sum of Value Added in all sectors

Adds up the value added at each stage of production to avoid double-counting. Value Added = Selling Price − Cost of Intermediate Inputs.

Example: Bread Production

Wheat farmer sells for ৳50 (VA=৳50) → Miller sells flour for ৳80 (VA=৳30) → Baker sells bread for ৳120 (VA=৳40). GDP contribution = ৳50+৳30+৳40 = ৳120 (not ৳250, which would be double-counting).

All Three Methods Should Give the Same Result

In theory, GDP (Expenditure) = GDP (Income) = GDP (Output). Any discrepancy is due to statistical errors.

Exam Tip Memorize the formula C + I + G + (X-M). Explain each component with examples. For output approach, clearly explain "value added" to show you understand why we don't add full sales price at each stage.
20
What is economic growth? Mention two determinants.
Definition

Economic Growth is the sustained increase in a country's productive capacity and real GDP over time. It means the economy can produce more goods and services, raising living standards for the population.

Measurement

Growth Rate = [(Real GDP₂ − Real GDP₁) ÷ Real GDP₁] × 100%

Determinant 1: Capital Accumulation (Investment)

Physical Capital

Investment in machinery, infrastructure, and technology increases productive capacity. More factories = more output per worker = higher GDP.

Human Capital

Investment in education, training, and healthcare improves worker productivity. A skilled workforce produces more efficiently — a core driver of Bangladesh's garment sector growth.

Determinant 2: Technological Progress

Innovation

New technology allows more output from the same inputs (higher Total Factor Productivity). Industrial Revolution, internet, and AI all dramatically expanded economic growth.

Research & Development

Countries that invest in R&D consistently achieve higher long-run growth rates. Technology is a key source of sustainable competitive advantage.

Bangladesh Example

Bangladesh achieved 6-7% annual real GDP growth by: (1) capital investment through remittances & FDI in RMG sector, and (2) technology adoption in manufacturing, digital banking (bKash), and agriculture (HYV seeds).

Exam Tip Also mention other determinants: natural resources, institutional quality (rule of law, property rights), trade openness. For the exam question that says "two determinants," deeply explain each one with examples rather than listing many superficially.

07
Discuss the characteristics of successful entrepreneurs.
Definition

An Entrepreneur is a person who identifies opportunities, takes calculated risks, organizes resources, and creates new businesses or innovations to generate value — accepting both the rewards and risks of doing so.

Key Characteristics

1. Vision & Innovation

Can see opportunities others miss. Steve Jobs saw that people wanted beautiful, intuitive computers before others did. Entrepreneurs think creatively and challenge the status quo.

2. Risk-Taking Ability

Willing to take calculated risks, not reckless ones. They assess probability of success, prepare backup plans, and move forward despite uncertainty.

3. Passion & Persistence

Deeply committed to their goals. They bounce back from failures (resilience). Elon Musk faced multiple SpaceX rocket failures before success.

4. Leadership & Motivation

Inspire and guide teams toward a shared vision. Can attract talented people and motivate them even without large salaries in early stages.

5. Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

Can make quick, effective decisions with incomplete information. They rely on both data analysis and intuition developed through experience.

6. Customer Focus

Always ask "what problem am I solving for my customer?" Successful entrepreneurs deeply understand customer pain points and build solutions around real needs.

7. Networking & Relationship Building

Build strong networks of mentors, investors, partners, and customers. "Your network is your net worth" — relationships open doors to opportunities and resources.

Bangladesh Example

Fazle Hasan Abed (BRAC founder) showed vision (tackling poverty through enterprise), persistence (rebuilt after 1971), innovation (microfinance + social enterprise model), and massive social impact — characteristics of a transformational entrepreneur.

Exam Tip The exam may expand this to: "Analyze which characteristics are most critical." Argue that vision + persistence are most fundamental — without vision you don't start, without persistence you don't finish.
08
Evaluate whether entrepreneurship is driven more by skills or innate traits.

The Debate: Nature vs Nurture in Entrepreneurship

This is one of the most important debates in entrepreneurship theory: Are entrepreneurs BORN (innate traits) or MADE (learned skills)?

Argument: Innate Traits Drive Entrepreneurship
  • Some people naturally have high risk tolerance
  • Creativity and intuition are partly genetic
  • Personality traits (openness, conscientiousness) are fairly stable
  • Many successful entrepreneurs showed traits from childhood
Argument: Skills Drive Entrepreneurship
  • Financial literacy can be learned
  • Marketing, management skills are teachable
  • Many entrepreneurs succeeded after formal training
  • Failure teaches practical wisdom
  • Serial entrepreneurs improve over time

Evaluation — The Balanced View

Traits Provide the Foundation

Traits like passion, risk tolerance, and curiosity create the entrepreneurial mindset — the "why" and "drive" to start. Without these, skills alone may not sustain entrepreneurship through adversity.

Skills Enable Execution

Having the drive without skills leads to failure. You need accounting, marketing, leadership, and strategic skills to survive. Entrepreneurship education programs (like this course) build these skills.

Conclusion: Both Are Required

Traits are necessary but NOT sufficient. Skills are learnable and can compensate for weaker traits. The most successful entrepreneurs have BOTH — natural drive enhanced by learned competencies. You don't need to be born an entrepreneur to become one.

Exam Tip "Evaluate" questions require you to present BOTH sides and then give a reasoned judgment. Don't just list — analyze strengths and weaknesses of each argument and conclude with your own reasoned position.
09
Explain the process of recognizing business opportunities with an example.
What is a Business Opportunity?

A business opportunity is a favorable set of circumstances that creates a need for a new product, service, or business. It must meet the 4 essential qualities: profitable, durable, timely, and anchored in a product/service that creates value.

Ways to Recognize Business Opportunities

1. Observe Problems & Pain Points

Every unsolved problem is a potential business opportunity. Entrepreneurs ask "what frustrates people?" — then build solutions. Uber solved the problem of unreliable taxi services.

2. Watch Market Trends

Emerging social, technological, economic, or demographic trends create new needs. Rise of remote work → demand for video conferencing (Zoom), home office furniture, cybersecurity.

3. Identify Unmet or Underserved Needs

Look for gaps where current products/services are inadequate. bKash identified that millions of Bangladeshis had no bank accounts but had mobile phones → mobile banking opportunity.

4. Use Brainstorming & Creativity Tools

Techniques like design thinking, SCAMPER, and mind mapping generate new business ideas by connecting existing concepts in novel ways.

5. Talk to Potential Customers

Direct customer interviews reveal unspoken needs. The best product ideas come from deeply understanding customer frustrations and aspirations.

4 Essential Qualities of a Good Opportunity

Attractive returns Durable (not temporary) Timely (right moment) Anchored in real value
Bangladesh Example

A student noticed elderly people struggled to get medicine from pharmacies during COVID lockdowns. She recognized the opportunity → launched an online medicine delivery service. Problem identified → opportunity validated → solution created → business started.

Exam Tip Always include a concrete example specific to Bangladesh or your local context. Show the PROCESS: problem observation → trend analysis → opportunity validation → business concept development.
10
Analyze why financial feasibility is critical before launching a startup.
Definition

Financial Feasibility Analysis determines whether a business idea can generate sufficient financial returns to justify the investment. It answers: "Can this business make money? Will it survive financially?"

Key Components of Financial Feasibility

1. Startup Cost Estimation

Calculate ALL costs to launch: equipment, rent, licenses, initial inventory, marketing, salaries. Underestimating startup costs is a leading cause of early startup failure.

2. Revenue Projection

Estimate realistic sales based on market size, target customers, and pricing. Avoid "hockey stick" projections. Conservative estimates with sensitivity analysis are more credible.

3. Breakeven Analysis

Determine how many units must be sold to cover all costs. Breakeven Point = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price − Variable Cost per unit). If breakeven is unachievable in a reasonable time, the business model is flawed.

4. Cash Flow Analysis

Even profitable businesses can fail if they run out of cash (cash flow crisis). Analyze WHEN money comes in vs when bills must be paid. Many startups fail not due to losses but due to cash timing mismatches.

5. Return on Investment (ROI)

Is the return worth the risk? Compare startup ROI with alternative investments. Investors require adequate risk premium for their capital.

Why It's CRITICAL — The Consequences of Skipping It

Run out of capital Unable to repay loans Forced closure Investor confidence lost Personal financial ruin
Real Scenario

A young entrepreneur opens a restaurant without financial feasibility analysis. He underestimates monthly rent + salaries + food costs. Monthly expenses = ৳200,000 but revenue = ৳150,000. He runs out of cash in 4 months and closes. A feasibility analysis would have revealed this fatal flaw BEFORE investing.

Exam Tip Include the formula: Breakeven Point = FC ÷ (P − VC). Argue that financial feasibility is the most critical feasibility type because even a great product with great marketing fails if the numbers don't work.
11
Discuss the essential elements of a business plan.
Definition

A Business Plan is a comprehensive written document that describes a business's goals, strategies, market analysis, operational plan, and financial projections. It serves as both a roadmap for the entrepreneur and a pitch document for investors.

Essential Elements

1. Executive Summary

A brief (1-2 page) overview of the entire plan. Written last but placed first. Must capture attention — investors often read only this and decide whether to continue.

2. Business Description

What the business does, its mission & vision, legal structure, location, and stage of development. Answers "What is this business?"

3. Market Analysis

Industry overview, target market size & demographics, competitor analysis (SWOT), market trends, and your unique position. Shows investors you understand your market.

4. Products/Services

Detailed description of what you offer, your value proposition, intellectual property, R&D pipeline. Why is it better than existing alternatives?

5. Marketing & Sales Strategy

How you will acquire and retain customers. Pricing strategy, distribution channels, promotional activities, and sales forecast.

6. Operations Plan

Day-to-day operations: production process, location, equipment, suppliers, quality control, and technology systems needed.

7. Management Team

Background and roles of founders and key team members. Investors often say "we invest in people, not just ideas." A strong team inspires confidence.

8. Financial Projections

3-5 year income statements, cash flow statements, balance sheets, break-even analysis, and funding requirements. Most critical section for investors.

Exam Tip Remember: Business plan serves TWO audiences — yourself (strategic clarity) and external parties (investors/lenders). Emphasize that financial projections and executive summary are the most scrutinized sections by investors.
12
Analyze potential weaknesses that can cause a business model to fail.
Business Model

A business model describes how a company creates, delivers, and captures value — how it makes money. Weaknesses in any of these dimensions can cause failure.

Key Weaknesses That Cause Failure

1. No Clear Value Proposition

If customers don't see why your product is better/different from alternatives, they won't switch. "Build it and they will come" is a fatal assumption. 42% of startups fail because there is no market need.

2. Poor Revenue Model / Unit Economics

If the cost to acquire a customer exceeds lifetime revenue from that customer (CAC > LTV), the business is fundamentally unprofitable. This is a structural flaw, not a marketing problem.

3. Cash Flow Problems

Running out of cash is the #1 operational killer. Even profitable businesses fail if receivables come slowly but payables are due immediately.

4. Wrong Team / Skill Gap

Missing key competencies (technical, financial, marketing) means the business cannot execute its plan. 23% of startups cite team problems as a key failure factor.

5. Underestimating Competition

Ignoring competitor responses to your entry. Established players can cut prices, copy features, or use distribution advantages to drive you out.

6. Scaling Too Fast or Too Slow

Growing before product-market fit → waste resources. Growing too slowly → miss the market window. Timing is critical.

7. Weak Distribution / Go-to-Market Strategy

A great product with no distribution plan fails. "Who will sell this and how?" must be answered before launch.

Exam Tip Use CB Insights research: "42% fail - no market need, 29% - ran out of cash, 23% - wrong team." These statistics add credibility to your analysis. Connect each weakness to a strategic failure mechanism.

Explain different types of startup firms.

Types of Startup Firms

1. Small Business Startup (Lifestyle Startup)

Started to provide income and a comfortable lifestyle for the owner. Not focused on high growth or external investment. Example: local restaurant, small retail shop, freelance consulting. Goal = sustainability and independence.

2. Scalable Startup (High-Growth Startup)

Designed to grow rapidly, disrupt markets, and attract venture capital. Example: Uber, Airbnb, Facebook, Pathao (Bangladesh). Goal = become a large company or unicorn. Often technology-driven.

3. Social Entrepreneurship Startup

Created primarily to solve social, environmental, or community problems — profit is secondary to mission. Example: BRAC, Grameen Bank (microfinance), solar energy cooperatives. Goal = social impact at scale.

4. Buyable Startup (Acquisition Target)

Built with the explicit goal of being acquired by a larger company. Often in tech — small teams build innovative features that big companies want. Example: Instagram was acquired by Facebook.

5. Large Company Startup (Intrapreneurship)

Innovation ventures within established companies. When a large firm creates a new division to explore disruptive markets. Example: Google's X lab, Samsung's new product divisions.

Exam Tip Distinguish clearly between lifestyle startups (sustainability goal) and scalable startups (growth/disruption goal). Mention that the type of startup determines its funding needs, team structure, and success metrics.

Evaluate brainstorming and design thinking as idea generation tools.

Brainstorming

Definition

Brainstorming is a group creativity technique where participants generate a large number of ideas freely without immediate criticism or judgment. Quantity is prioritized over quality in the first stage.

Strengths

Fast, inclusive, generates many diverse ideas, encourages participation, breaks mental blocks. Works well for initial idea generation phases.

Weaknesses

Risk of groupthink (conforming to dominant ideas), loudest voices dominate, quantity doesn't guarantee quality, ideas may be disconnected from real customer needs.

Design Thinking

5-Stage Process

Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test. Starts with deep understanding of the user's experience and needs, then builds solutions around those needs.

Strengths

Customer-centric, produces validated solutions, reduces risk of building the wrong product, promotes iteration and learning from failure. Used by Apple, IDEO, and leading innovators.

Weaknesses

Time-consuming (requires extensive user research), needs diverse team with empathy skills, may be too structured for highly creative contexts.

Evaluation: Which is More Effective?

Both tools serve different purposes and are most powerful when combined: use brainstorming for rapid divergent thinking, then design thinking for user-centered refinement and validation. Design thinking is superior for building sustainable businesses because it grounds ideas in real customer needs.

Exam Tip "Evaluate" = assess strengths AND weaknesses of both, then reach a comparative judgment. Always mention: "The best approach combines both tools at different stages of the idea development process."

Discuss the importance of intellectual property protection for startups.
Definition

Intellectual Property (IP) refers to creations of the mind — inventions, designs, brand names, artistic works, and trade secrets — that can be legally protected from unauthorized use.

Types of IP Protection

Patent (inventions) Trademark (brand name/logo) Copyright (creative works) Trade Secret (confidential processes)

Why IP Protection is Critical for Startups

1. Competitive Advantage

Patents prevent competitors from copying your core technology for 20 years. This time gives startups space to build market position. Without patent protection, large firms immediately copy innovations.

2. Investor Attractiveness

Investors value startups with protected IP much more highly. IP demonstrates proprietary technology that justifies premium valuations and protects returns on investment.

3. Brand Value & Trust

Trademarks protect your brand name and logo. As you build reputation, your brand becomes valuable — IP protection prevents others from riding on your brand equity.

4. Revenue Generation Through Licensing

IP can be licensed to other companies for royalty income — an additional revenue stream. Some companies generate more from licensing IP than from direct product sales.

5. Prevents Idea Theft by Employees/Partners

Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) and registered IP prevent former employees or business partners from using your proprietary knowledge to compete against you.

Exam Tip Use specific example: Grameen Phone's brand trademark protects its identity. A small tech startup's patented algorithm is its most valuable asset. IP turns intangible ideas into legally protected, commercially valuable assets.

Explain the role of board of directors and advisors in a new venture.
Board of Directors
  • Legal governing body of company
  • Appointed by shareholders
  • Has fiduciary duties (loyalty, care)
  • Approves major decisions
  • Hires & fires CEO
  • Reviews financial statements
  • Sets strategic direction
Advisory Board
  • Informal, no legal obligation
  • Chosen for expertise & network
  • Provide guidance & mentorship
  • Open doors to investors/partners
  • No binding authority
  • Usually compensated with equity
  • Industry-specific knowledge

Why These Roles Matter for Startups

Experience Gap Filling

Young entrepreneurs lack experience in finance, law, HR, and strategy. Board members and advisors fill these gaps with real-world expertise without the cost of full-time hires.

Accountability Structure

Board of Directors prevents the entrepreneur from making unchecked decisions that could harm shareholders. This governance structure is required by investors.

Network Access

Advisors with industry connections open doors to investors, key customers, strategic partners, and media — resources that are otherwise inaccessible to new ventures.

Credibility Signal

A board with recognized names signals to the market that credible, experienced people believe in the venture. This credibility helps with fundraising and customer acquisition.

Exam Tip Distinguish clearly: Directors have LEGAL authority and fiduciary duties; Advisors have PERSUASIVE influence only. Both add value in different ways. A startup should build both as it grows.

Analyze common myths about entrepreneurship.

Major Myths and the Reality

Myth 1: "Entrepreneurs are born, not made"

Reality: While some personality traits help, entrepreneurship skills are largely learnable. Many successful entrepreneurs had no natural advantage — they developed skills through education, mentorship, and deliberate practice. This course itself is evidence that entrepreneurship can be taught.

Myth 2: "You need a completely new/unique idea"

Reality: Most successful businesses are improvements on existing ideas. Facebook wasn't the first social network. Google wasn't the first search engine. Execution, timing, and understanding customer needs matter far more than radical novelty.

Myth 3: "Entrepreneurship is all about being your own boss / freedom"

Reality: Entrepreneurs often work longer hours, face more stress, and have less security than employees — especially in early stages. The "freedom" comes only after building a sustainable business, which takes years of sacrifice.

Myth 4: "You need a lot of money to start a business"

Reality: Many successful businesses started with minimal capital. bKash started with an idea and partnerships. Many service businesses (consulting, coding, tutoring) require almost no startup capital. Creativity and hustle can substitute for capital in early stages.

Myth 5: "Failure means you are not an entrepreneur"

Reality: Most successful entrepreneurs failed multiple times. Elon Musk's first three SpaceX rockets failed. Henry Ford went bankrupt twice. Failure provides invaluable learning that cannot be taught in classrooms. Silicon Valley celebrates "fail fast, learn fast."

Myth 6: "Entrepreneurs are lone wolves / solo heroes"

Reality: The most successful ventures are built by teams, not individuals. Jobs had Wozniak. Entrepreneurship requires collaboration, delegation, and building systems — not doing everything alone.

Why Debunking Myths Matters

These myths act as barriers that prevent capable people from pursuing entrepreneurship. Understanding the reality empowers more people — regardless of background, capital, or personality type — to create businesses that generate economic and social value.

Exam Tip For an "analyze" question on myths, don't just list them — explain WHY each myth is dangerous and what the TRUE understanding leads to in terms of better entrepreneurial behavior. This shows depth of analysis.
